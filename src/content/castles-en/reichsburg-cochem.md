## History

Reichsburg Cochem is one of the oldest and most significant castle complexes on the Moselle. Its origins reach back to the early eleventh century, when the site first appears in records as a fortified stronghold of the Imperial Ministeriales. At that time it served as an important administrative and power base in the middle Moselle valley – a region already regarded in Roman times as a strategic corridor between the Rhine provinces and the western parts of the empire.

In 1151, Emperor Conrad III pledged the castle to the Archbishop of Cologne, heralding the beginning of a turbulent history of ownership. Over the centuries, lordship and pledgeship changed hands repeatedly until King Rudolf of Habsburg brought the complex back under imperial influence in 1282. During the Thirty Years' War, Cochem and its castle suffered severe devastation, and in 1689, during the War of the Palatine Succession, French troops under Marshal de Créqui almost completely destroyed the Reichsburg. For nearly two centuries it lay as a ruin, abandoned to decay.

## Rebuilding and the Wilhelmine Era

The appearance Reichsburg Cochem presents today is owed to a Berlin merchant named Louis Ravené, who purchased the ruin in 1868 and commissioned a comprehensive rebuilding programme. The work lasted until 1877 and was directed principally by the Cologne architect Julius Raschdorff. The new structure drew on the Romantic historicism of the Gründerzeit – an era in which medieval castles were staged as symbols of national identity and chivalric virtue. The result is an impressive synthesis of authentically preserved masonry and Neo-Gothic forms, which together give the complex its imposing appearance today.

## Architecture & Setting

The Reichsburg rises on a steeply falling rocky spur some 80 metres above the Moselle, commanding Cochem's skyline in a wholly unique way. The irregular ground plan follows the natural topography of the hillspur and is divided into several building bodies: a mighty keep, a palas containing the principal residential chambers, and various service and defensive structures. The tall watch-towers with their pointed helmets, the rugged curtain walls and the widely visible keep define the character of the complex and reflect the style of the high-medieval hilltop castle.

Inside, the elaborately appointed rooms from the rebuilding phase captivate visitors: wood-panelled halls, historic suits of armour and weapons, tapestries and representative furniture convey the atmosphere of a late-medieval residence. From the castle courtyard and the upper wall-walks, spectacular views open out over the Moselle's sweeping meanders, the wooded hillsides and the picturesque vineyards of the Moselle valley – a panorama that draws visitors from across Europe.

## Visiting & Tips

Reichsburg Cochem is open daily from March to November and may only be visited on guided tours, which depart hourly and last approximately 40 minutes. Tours are offered in German and, on request, in English as well. Those wishing to delve deeper into the castle's history will find special tours during the summer months covering topics such as castle defence and everyday medieval life.

For the journey, arriving by train is highly recommended: Cochem lies on the busy Moselle Railway line between Koblenz and Trier, and the station is easily reached on foot from the market square. From there, a well-maintained footpath leads up to the castle. The town of Cochem itself is also well worth a stroll – the historic town hall, the Marktgasse and the many wine taverns invite a thoroughly enjoyable stay. Combined with a Moselle wine tasting or a boat trip on the river, a visit makes for an unforgettable day out in one of Germany's most beautiful upland landscapes.
